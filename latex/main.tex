\chapter*{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{empty}
% =====================================================================

``Styles make fights'' is the most-repeated cliché in mixed martial
arts.  Statistical models of UFC outcomes almost never encode style
explicitly: they regress wins on per-minute strike and grapple rates
together with physical and experiential covariates, and whatever
residual structure style contributes is left to the classifier to
exploit.  This thesis asks whether the cliché survives a rigorous
test---whether latent fighting styles can be recovered directly from
per-fight data, and whether, once recovered, they sharpen our
predictions of \emph{who} wins, \emph{how} the fight unfolds, and
\emph{when} a favourite is vulnerable.  Using a leakage-free pipeline
over roughly seven thousand UFC bouts through 2024, we learn soft
Gaussian mixture posteriors at $K=3$ and $K=5$ and a
three-dimensional autoencoder embedding and validate them against a
curated panel of twenty-five champions: Khabib Nurmagomedov, Islam
Makhachev, and Georges St-Pierre all embed in the takedown-heavy
wrestler cluster, while Max Holloway, Jose Aldo, Conor McGregor,
Israel Adesanya, and Alex Pereira all embed in the active
distance-striker cluster.

The answers to the three questions differ sharply in kind.  For
\emph{who wins}, a stack of six model families on the full non-market
feature set lifts held-out AUC from $0.600$ to $0.670$ above a
$Z$-score baseline, and a version that includes Vegas as a feature
edges the market from AUC $0.751$ to $0.775$ with a paired bootstrap
95\% CI on the difference that excludes zero on all three of AUC,
Brier, and log loss ($p = 0.04,\ 0.02,\ 0.01$); a paired null finding
is that hybrid generalists do not systematically outperform
specialists on win rate.  For \emph{how the fight unfolds},
top-quintile autoencoder style divergence raises the observed finish
rate from $\approx 0.47$ to $\approx 0.56$, and a hierarchical
$P(\text{finish}) \cdot P(\text{method} \mid \text{finish}) \cdot
P(\text{winner} \mid \text{method})$ decomposition cuts six-way log
loss from $2.15$ to $1.94$.  For \emph{when a favourite is
vulnerable}, the autoencoder group is the largest non-market
contributor to a residual detector
$r = p_{\text{model}} - p_{\text{vegas},A}$, and a de-vigged
flat-stake ROI simulation returns $+17.5\%$ on $411$ bets at
$\theta = 0.15$ with a bootstrap 95\% CI of $[+1.5\%,\ +34.1\%]$.
Style, in short, does help ``make fights,'' but in a more specific
sense than the cliché is usually stated: it helps least where MMA
analysts most often invoke it (who wins) and most where they rarely
do (how fights end and when markets are wrong).

\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:introduction}
% =====================================================================

``Styles make fights'' is the most-quoted cliché in mixed martial
arts.  Broadcasters explain outcomes through the language of
archetypes---striker against grappler, pressure fighter against
counter-puncher---and attribute results to the stylistic pairing
rather than to raw quality.  Statistical work on UFC outcomes has been
slow to encode this intuition.  The standard pipeline regresses wins
on per-minute striking rates, takedown accuracy, control time,
physical attributes, and recent form
\citep{hitkul2019mma,yin2024mma,sheng2024ufc}; each
feature enters as an independent input and whatever structure
``style'' contributes is left to the downstream classifier to
discover implicitly.  When style does enter explicitly, it does so
through hand-applied labels---``striker,'' ``grappler,''
``hybrid''---that analysts disagree about and that cannot represent
continuous variation in fighting behaviour.  This thesis asks whether
latent fighting styles can instead be recovered directly from
per-fight data, and whether, once recovered, they sharpen our
predictions of \emph{who} wins a fight, \emph{how} the fight unfolds,
and \emph{when} a favourite is vulnerable.

The three questions are ordered by the difficulty with which they can
be answered in the affirmative.  Predicting wins in a combat sport
with a roughly $55\%$ finish rate and large fighter-to-fighter
variance is hard, and the betting market already integrates much
public and private information.  Predicting how a fight unfolds is a
richer task with more outcome categories in which style can leave a
fingerprint.  Identifying upsets is easier still if genuine style
effects are concentrated in the tails of mismatched matchups---where
a favourite looks dominant by every numeric feature but is
stylistically exposed.  The rest of the thesis finds that this
ordering is exactly right.  For \emph{who wins}, a stack of six model
families on the full non-market feature set lifts held-out AUC from
$0.600$ to $0.670$ above a $Z$-score baseline and, with de-vigged
Vegas probabilities added as a feature, edges the market itself by a
small but statistically significant margin.  For \emph{how fights
unfold}, top-quintile autoencoder style divergence raises the
observed finish rate by roughly eight percentage points, and a
hierarchical method-of-victory decomposition cuts six-way log loss by
$\sim 10\%$ versus a flat classifier.  For \emph{when a favourite is
vulnerable}, a Vegas-aware residual upset model in which the
autoencoder embedding is the single largest non-market contributor
returns a de-vigged ROI of $+17.5\%$ on $411$ simulated bets at
threshold $\theta = 0.15$ with a paired bootstrap
\citep{efron1979bootstrap} $95\%$ CI of $[+1.5\%,\ +34.1\%]$.  Style,
in short, does help make fights, but in a more specific sense than
the cliché is usually stated: it helps least where MMA analysts most
often invoke it (who wins) and most where they rarely do (how fights
end and when markets are wrong).

\chapter{Background and Related Work}\label{ch:background}
% =====================================================================

The peer-reviewed literature on supervised UFC outcome prediction
is small.  \citet{hitkul2019mma} present an early comparative study
that evaluates random forests \citep{breiman2001random}, decision
trees, a perceptron, stochastic-gradient-descent logistic regression,
support vector machines, and $k$-nearest neighbours on a UFC scrape,
reporting roughly $63\%$ accuracy on a random hold-out.  More
recently, \citet{sheng2024ufc} benchmark random forests, gradient
boosted decision trees, and SVMs on the Kaggle ``UFC-Master'' dataset
and identify age difference and fight date as their most influential
features.  Closest in spirit to the present work,
\citet{yin2024mma} apply factor analysis to per-fight statistics,
cluster fighters into a handful of technical styles with $k$-means,
and feed the style assignment into an ensemble of logistic regression,
random forest, SVM, XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost}, and a shallow
neural network, reaching $65.5\%$ accuracy and showing in an ablation
that the style factor contributes positively.  None of these studies
use a chronological split, a calibrated probabilistic baseline, or a
continuous style embedding, and none compare their predictions to
market odds.  A recurring pitfall across the body of work is
\emph{data leakage}: career-aggregate features computed on the full
dataset yield optimistically biased test metrics when the aggregation
window includes the fight being predicted.  The present thesis avoids
this by computing rolling pre-fight statistics walked in strict
chronological order (Chapter~\ref{ch:data}).

Two rating systems appear as features throughout.  Elo
\citep{elo1978rating} updates each competitor after every bout based
on the actual versus expected outcome; the rating difference, passed
through a logistic CDF, yields a win probability.  Glicko-2
\citep{glickman1999parameter,glickman2012example} extends Elo with a
rating deviation and a volatility term and behaves better for
fighters with layoffs or small samples.  Both are walked
chronologically, so the rating used to predict a fight is strictly
the rating before it resolves.

For style discovery we use two complementary representations.
Gaussian mixture models
\citep{bishop2006pattern,hastie2009elements,dempster1977em} are the
soft-clustering counterpart of $K$-means: each fighter is associated
with a posterior $\pi = (\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_K)$ over components
rather than a hard label, so hybrid fighters are represented by a
spread posterior rather than by an arbitrary hard assignment; the
Shannon entropy $H(\pi) = -\sum_k \pi_k \log \pi_k$ summarises a
fighter's ``genericity'' in a single scalar (the \emph{Hybrid Score}).
A feed-forward autoencoder \citep{hinton2006reducing} implemented in
PyTorch \citep{paszke2019pytorch} and optimised with Adam
\citep{kingma2014adam} embeds each qualified fighter into
$\mathbb{R}^3$; differences and means of the two corners' embeddings
enter downstream models as matchup features.  The direct transfer of
GMMs and autoencoders to per-fighter MMA profiles is, to our
knowledge, novel.

Downstream, probability calibration matters whenever a model is used
for betting or resource allocation.  We use isotonic regression
\citep{zadrozny2002transforming,niculescu2005predicting} both as a
post-hoc calibrator on individual models and as the meta-learner in
a stacked ensemble \citep{wolpert1992stacked,breiman1996stacked};
calibration quality is reported as expected calibration error
\citep{guo2017calibration} alongside the Brier score
\citep{brier1950verification}.  For significance testing on paired
metrics we use the nonparametric paired bootstrap
\citep{efron1979bootstrap}, which yields percentile CIs and two-sided
$p$-values without parametric assumptions.  For interpretability we
report \emph{grouped} permutation importance
\citep{breiman2001random} over semantic
feature families---rolling rates, style posteriors, embeddings,
ratings, physicals, Vegas---shuffling an entire group rather than
individual columns, which is more legible than SHAP when group
members are colinear.  All pipelines are implemented in scikit-learn
\citep{scikitlearn2011}, with XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost} and
LightGBM \citep{ke2017lightgbm} used where noted.

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Data and Preprocessing}\label{ch:data}
% =====================================================================

The primary dataset is the \emph{UFC Fights, Fighters, and Events}
Kaggle scrape of UFCStats \citep{ufckagglealibi}: fight-level
metadata (date, method, round, duration), per-fight positional
striking and grappling statistics, and fighter-level attributes
(date of birth, height, reach, stance, weight class).  After
cleaning, the merged table contains roughly seven thousand fights
from 1993 through 2024.  For the Vegas baselines we use a
community-maintained betting-odds dump \citep{ufcoddskaggle}, filtered
to US moneylines and one row per fight, with de-vigged implied
probabilities $p_{\text{vegas},A} = o_A^{-1} / (o_A^{-1} + o_B^{-1})$.
Vegas coverage is partial ($\approx 40\%$ of all fights,
$\approx 60\%$ of test-era fights), so every Vegas-using analysis
below is restricted to the Vegas-aligned subset.

The preprocessing pipeline is built around a rolling pre-fight
aggregator that snapshots each fighter's cumulative career averages
\emph{before} incorporating the current bout.  Output columns include
pre-fight per-minute rates (significant strikes, takedowns,
submission attempts, control ratio, positional striking shares), KO
and submission rates for and against, and five-fight recent form.
Because the aggregates at fight $t$ strictly exclude fight $t$ and
all subsequent fights, rolling columns are never contaminated by the
outcome they are used to predict---the single most common
optimistic-bias bug in published MMA models.  Per-minute rates are
$Z$-scored \emph{within weight class} to remove division-specific
baselines.  Elo ratings are walked with a standard specification
(initial $1500$, $K = 24$, logistic scale $400$;
\citealp{elo1978rating}) and Glicko-2 with monthly rating periods and
$\tau = 0.5$ \citep{glickman2012example}; pre-fight $(R_A, R_B)$,
their difference, and Glicko-2 rating deviations are attached as
features.

A single artefact, \texttt{ufc\_matchup\_features.csv}, gathers every
column used downstream: identifiers, rolling rates for both corners
plus $\delta$ and $\mu$ transforms, $K{=}3$ and $K{=}5$ GMM
posteriors and Hybrid Scores, the three-dimensional autoencoder
embedding, physicals and weight-class one-hots, Elo and Glicko-2
ratings, an empirical style-pair heatmap probability
$p_{\text{heatmap},A}$ (train-only win rate of A's $K{=}5$ cluster
versus B's), and $p_{\text{vegas},A}$ where available.  A
\texttt{split} column $\in \{\text{train}, \text{val},
\text{test}\}$ from an event-ordered $60/20/20$ cut ensures every
downstream model shares the same training, validation, and test
events.  All test-slice metrics are reported on the most recent
$20\%$ of events; model selection and isotonic calibration happen on
\texttt{val}; cross-validation within \texttt{train} uses
walk-forward folds.  Every training row is augmented with its A/B
mirror so models cannot exploit the arbitrary corner convention, and
test metrics are reported on unmirrored rows so each fight counts
once.

Figure~\ref{fig:matchup-corr} anchors what follows.  Across the
matchup table, significant-strike-volume delta is the strongest raw
correlate of the outcome ($\rho \approx 0.19$); reach and height
deltas carry weaker signal ($\rho \approx 0.07$ and $0.03$); and a
naïve hand-engineered ``style clash'' score correlates at essentially
zero with who wins.  This foreshadows the thesis's central pattern:
style will matter more for \emph{how} a fight unfolds than for the
top-line outcome.

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Discovering Fighting Styles}\label{ch:style}
% =====================================================================

Style discovery operates on a fighter-level profile $x \in
\mathbb{R}^7$ summarising each qualified fighter's career: the
$Z$-scored per-minute rates of significant strikes, takedown
attempts, and submission attempts, the control-time ratio, and the
three positional shares of striking (distance, clinch, ground).
Fighters with fewer than five UFC bouts are excluded as too noisy to
support a stable assignment.  The same seven features feed all three
representations below---$K$-means, Gaussian mixtures, and an
autoencoder---so that any difference between representations reflects
model choice rather than feature engineering.

\section{GMM posteriors and autoencoder embeddings}

A $K$-means baseline with $K \in \{4,5,6\}$ selects $K = 5$ by
silhouette \citep{rousseeuw1987silhouettes} and serves downstream
only as the backing label set of the style-pair heatmap below.  The
representations we actually use in Chapters~\ref{ch:matchup}--\ref{ch:cases}
are soft.  Gaussian mixtures with full covariance are fit at
$K \in \{2,\ldots,8\}$ and selected by AIC/BIC
\citep{schwarz1978bic}; we persist $K=3$ and $K=5$ posteriors
$\pi = (\pi_1,\ldots,\pi_K)$ via the EM algorithm
\citep{dempster1977em} together with the Hybrid Score
$H(\pi) = -\sum_k \pi_k \log \pi_k$.  Low $H$ flags specialists with
near-degenerate posteriors; high $H$ flags generalists whose mass is
spread across components.  A feed-forward autoencoder
$7 \to 32 \to 3 \to 32 \to 7$ with ReLU activations, trained with
Adam \citep{kingma2014adam} to minimise reconstruction MSE in
PyTorch \citep{paszke2019pytorch}, provides a continuous companion
representation.  The two are complementary, not redundant: roughly
$70\%$ of GMM posterior mass concentrates near simplex vertices, so
the mixture flavours its output as discrete-with-softening; the
autoencoder supplies a smooth $\mathbb{R}^3$ space where hybrids
interpolate between archetypes rather than collapsing to a single
``hybrid'' vertex.

Figure~\ref{fig:gmm-profiles} shows the $Z$-score centroid profile of
each $K=5$ component.  The five clusters are interpretable:
\textbf{Cluster 0} ($n=60$) is the submission-leaning grappler
($+1.36\sigma$ submissions, $-0.86\sigma$ strikes);
\textbf{Cluster 1} ($n=279$) is the active distance striker
($+0.56\sigma$ strike volume);
\textbf{Cluster 2} ($n=53$) is low-activity across the board;
\textbf{Cluster 3} ($n=506$) is the baseline generalist, near zero
on every primary rate and by far the largest group;
\textbf{Cluster 4} ($n=292$) is the takedown-heavy wrestler
($+0.91\sigma$ takedowns, $+0.89\sigma$ control,
$+0.84\sigma$ submissions).

\section{Champion validation}\label{sec:champion-validation}

A silhouette score cannot tell a reader whether our representations
agree with how knowledgeable fans describe fighters.  To answer that
question we curate a panel of twenty-five UFC champions spanning
weight classes and eras, annotate each with a short expert label,
and check how they land in the autoencoder space
(Figure~\ref{fig:ae-champions}).  Three patterns emerge.
\emph{First,} fighters described as universally similar cluster
together: Khabib Nurmagomedov, Islam Makhachev, and Georges St-Pierre
all land in Cluster~4 (takedown wrestler) with Amanda Nunes and
Randy Couture, and Max Holloway, Jose Aldo, Conor McGregor, Israel
Adesanya, and Alex Pereira all land in Cluster~1 (distance striker).
\emph{Second,} stylistically distinct era-defining champions
separate: Khabib, Holloway, and Francis Ngannou fall into three
distinct $K=5$ clusters.  \emph{Third,} the Hybrid Score ranks
generalists at the top of the panel: Georges St-Pierre, Demetrious
Johnson, Henry Cejudo, and Daniel Cormier---the four fighters most
routinely cited as the sport's most complete generalists---sit in
the upper quintile of $H(\pi)$, while Khabib ($H \approx 0.07$) and
Ngannou ($H \approx 0.21$) sit at the bottom.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{ae_champions.png}
\caption{Three-dimensional autoencoder style space with twenty-five
annotated UFC champions, coloured by $K=5$ hard assignment.  Khabib,
Makhachev, and GSP cluster with other wrestler-dominant fighters;
Holloway, Aldo, McGregor, Adesanya, and Pereira cluster with other
kickboxing-style strikers; generalists such as Jon Jones,
Volkanovski, Johnson, and Cejudo fall in the baseline cluster

\label{fig:ae-champions}

Two observations are worth flagging for later chapters.  First, the
champion panel is dominated by Cluster~3: twelve of the twenty-five
champions (Jones, Volkanovski, Dillashaw, Cejudo, Johnson, Ngannou,
Cormier, Cruz, Usman, Edwards, Poirier, Weili) sit there.  The modal
archetype of a modern UFC champion is not ``striker'' or
``wrestler'' but \emph{generalist}.  Second, the representation
occasionally contradicts the fan label.  Ngannou---described by fans
as a one-shot power puncher and ``expected'' to cluster with the
strikers---lands in Cluster~3 because his fights end so quickly
(average duration under six minutes) that his per-minute striking and
grappling rates regress to the population mean.  This is an honest
quirk of the feature space: per-minute normalisation is what makes
the representation comparable across career lengths, and the price
we pay is that a fighter whose identity is ``one KO per fight in the
first round'' looks average on per-minute rates.

\section{Style-pair interactions and a first null result}\label{sec:heatmap}

With hard $K=5$ labels in hand we tabulate the empirical win rate
$w_{ij}$ of Cluster~$i$ versus Cluster~$j$
(Figure~\ref{fig:style-heatmap}).  The heatmap is predominantly near
$0.5$: style alone is a weak unconditional predictor of the outcome,
consistent with the near-zero correlation of the ``style clash''
score with winning in \fref{fig:matchup-corr}.  Off-diagonal cells
display larger asymmetries, but the counts behind those cells are
small (the largest cell-level asymmetry involves only a few dozen
fights), so we treat the heatmap as a descriptive baseline rather
than as a significance-tested hypothesis.  Its row probabilities are
attached to the unified matchup table as the $p_{\text{heatmap},A}$
feature.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{style_heatmap.png}

A related and sharper test asks whether hybrid generalists
systematically outperform specialists because their opponents
``cannot game-plan'' against them.  Binning fighters into quintiles
of $H(\pi)$ at $K=5$ (Figure~\ref{fig:hybrid-winrate}) produces no
bucket more than $1.5$ percentage points from $50\%$.  At the
population level the hybrid-beats-specialist hypothesis is
\emph{not} supported.  The null is sharper than it looks: the same
question for \emph{how} a fight ends and for \emph{when} a favourite
is mispriced will return real, positive effects later, so what the
data rule out is specifically the claim that hybridness translates
into higher win rates in the aggregate---not the broader claim that
style is informative in the right task.

\chapter{Does Style Predict Who Wins?}\label{ch:matchup}
% =====================================================================

The first of the thesis's three questions is whether style sharpens
prediction of \emph{who} wins.  The answer developed here is: yes,
but at the margin.  A stack of six model families on the full
non-market feature set lifts held-out AUC from $0.600$ to $0.670$
over a $Z$-score-delta baseline, and a Vegas-aware version of the
same stack edges the betting market itself on AUC, Brier, and log
loss.  Neither result displaces the market; what they establish is
that style carries marginal predictive content beyond classical
features, and that the content survives when Vegas is already
present---the condition under which residual upset prediction in
Chapter~\ref{ch:upset} becomes possible.

\section{Model families and the ablation ladder}

Notebook~19 fits six supervised classifiers on the unified matchup
table: logistic regression with $\ell_2$ regularisation; random
forests \citep{breiman2001random}; histogram-based gradient boosting
(\citealp{friedman2001greedy}; scikit-learn
\citealp{scikitlearn2011}); XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost}; LightGBM
\citep{ke2017lightgbm}; and a two-layer MLP trained in PyTorch
\citep{paszke2019pytorch} with Adam \citep{kingma2014adam}.
Hyperparameters are tuned on \texttt{val} and frozen before touching
\texttt{test}; non-probabilistic outputs are isotonically calibrated
\citep{zadrozny2002transforming} on \texttt{val}.

To quantify the marginal contribution of each feature family, every
model is trained on a sequence of nested feature sets that add one
group at a time.  Table~\ref{tab:ablation} reports held-out test AUC
and Brier for the best single learner at each step.

The non-Vegas portion of the ladder (\texttt{z\_only} to
\texttt{full}) lifts AUC from $0.600$ to $\approx 0.670$: GMM
posteriors ($+0.03$), autoencoder embeddings ($+0.01$), and rolling
pre-fight rates ($+0.03$) are the three non-trivial contributors;
physicals and weight-class one-hots add nothing further after
$Z$-scoring.  The step from \texttt{full} to \texttt{full+vegas} is
the largest single step in the ladder ($0.670 \to 0.775$) and is the
one that takes the stack above the market.

$\Delta$Brier    & \multicolumn{3}{l}{$-0.010$\ \ 95\% CI $[-0.018,\ -0.001]$,\ $p = 0.02$} \\
$\Delta$log loss & \multicolumn{3}{l}{$-0.025$\ \ 95\% CI $[-0.046,\ -0.004]$,\ $p = 0.01$} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Stack+Vegas beats Vegas alone on all three metrics at $p \le 0.05$,
with 95\% paired bootstrap CIs that exclude zero in every case.  The
effect is small but not within sampling noise.  Equally important,
the pure non-market stack (``Stack'') is \emph{worse} than Vegas on
every metric ($\Delta$AUC $= -0.054$ with 95\% CI
$[-0.093,\ -0.017]$).  The positive result is therefore not ``style
beats the book on its own'' but the sharper claim that style
contributes information the book does not already incorporate.  The
reliability curves in Figure~\ref{fig:reliability-vegas} show the
same story geometrically: \texttt{Stack+Vegas} tracks the diagonal
more closely than Vegas alone and attains lower Brier, log loss, and
ECE, while the Vegas-free models sit below the diagonal at short
odds and above it at long odds.  Grouped permutation importance on
the stacked model (reprised for the upset model in
Chapter~\ref{ch:upset}) confirms that $p_{\text{vegas},A}$ is by far
the single most important column; among non-market features, the
rolling pre-fight rates and the autoencoder embedding are the two
largest contributors.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{reliability_vegas.png}
\caption{Left: reliability curves on the Vegas-aligned test subset
for Vegas, HGB (non-Vegas features), logistic regression (full), and

\texttt{Stack+Vegas} tracks the diagonal most closely and attains

On \emph{who wins}, then, style is a supporting actor: it lifts the
feature-stack AUC by a few points above a $Z$-score baseline, is
strictly dominated by Vegas on its own, and beats Vegas only by a
margin whose CIs make it hard to dismiss but difficult to trumpet.
Combined with the hybrid-versus-specialist null of
Section~\ref{sec:heatmap}, the natural question is where, if
anywhere, style decisively matters.  The next two chapters argue the
answer is \emph{how} the fight unfolds and \emph{when} a favourite
is vulnerable.

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Does Style Predict How Fights Unfold?}\label{ch:dynamics}
% =====================================================================

If \emph{who} wins is the hardest of the three questions, \emph{how}
a fight unfolds is the richest: duration, method of victory, and
strike and grapple volume are all dimensions along which style can
leave a fingerprint.  This chapter reports two findings---a
hierarchical factorisation of the method of victory, and the
thesis's cleanest positive style effect in the
style-divergence-to-finish-rate curve.

A flat six-way classifier over \{\texttt{A\_KO}, \texttt{A\_SUB},
\texttt{A\_DEC}, \texttt{B\_KO}, \texttt{B\_SUB}, \texttt{B\_DEC}\}
must jointly learn ``will there be a finish?'' and ``who finishes
whom?'' from a single softmax.  We instead factor
\[
P(\text{outcome}) = P(\text{finish}) \cdot
P(\text{method}\mid\text{finish}) \cdot
P(\text{winner}\mid\text{method}),
\]
and train one histogram-based gradient booster
\citep{friedman2001greedy} for each conditional.  On the held-out
test slice the hierarchical model attains log loss $1.94$ versus
$2.15$ for the flat six-way HGB, a $10\%$ reduction.  The largest
per-cell improvement is on the rare submission classes, which we
attribute to the structure letting the model borrow strength from
the common binary finish head rather than having to learn
submission-specific probabilities from a handful of examples.

The chapter's headline plot is the relationship between
\emph{autoencoder style divergence}---the Euclidean distance
$\lVert z_A - z_B \rVert_2$ between the two corners' embeddings---and
the realised finish rate (Figure~\ref{fig:style-divergence-finish}).
Across test-slice quintiles of style divergence, $P(\text{finish})$
is essentially flat near $0.47$ in the bottom three quintiles, rises
modestly in quintile~4, and jumps to $0.56$ in quintile~5.  The
top-quintile finish rate is roughly eight percentage points above
the bottom three; the effect persists after controlling for
Weight\_Class and Elo differential in a logistic regression.  The
honest interpretation is not ``monotone in divergence'' (the bottom
three bins are flat) but rather \emph{the tail of stylistically
mismatched fights finishes noticeably more often}, matching the
intuition that the most decisive bouts happen when one fighter's
style exploits the other's core weakness.  As a sanity check,
binary-finish Brier on the Vegas-aligned test subset is $0.26$ for
our model versus $0.35$ for Vegas' implied finish probabilities
(derived from its round-props markets), so the effect is not merely
recoverable from the market.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{style_divergence_finish.png}
\caption{Observed finish rate by quintile of autoencoder style
divergence $\lVert z_A - z_B \rVert_2$ on held-out test events.
The bottom three quintiles sit near $0.47$; quintile~5 reaches
$0.56$, an eight-percentage-point lift concentrated in the tail of
maximally mismatched matchups.  Source: notebook~21.}
\label{fig:style-divergence-finish}
\end{figure}

Continuous dynamics targets split cleanly into predictable and
stubborn: takedowns ($R^2 \approx 0.18$) and submission attempts
($R^2 \approx 0.15$) are meaningfully regressable on pre-fight
features, while striking totals ($R^2 \approx 0.08$) and fight
duration ($R^2 < 0$ against a weight-class-mean baseline) are
dominated by round-level variance.  Grappling activity is a stable
career trait; striking totals are heavy-tailed in round-level shot
counts.  On \emph{how} fights unfold, style is a first-class signal
wherever the dimension of variation is structural (finish rate,
method of victory, takedown volume) and a bit-player wherever it is
noise-driven (strike totals, duration).  The next chapter asks
whether this same structural signal can be exploited where a sports
model can be both economically and inferentially honest: in
identifying fights the market has mispriced.

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Does Style Help Catch Upsets?}\label{ch:upset}
% =====================================================================

Chapter~\ref{ch:matchup} established that a Vegas-aware stack edges
Vegas alone by a small aggregate margin.  Chapter~\ref{ch:dynamics}
established that the autoencoder embedding does most of the style
work on fight dynamics.  The question for this chapter is whether
the two compose: does a Vegas-aware model that uses the autoencoder
embedding among its features systematically flag upset candidates
the market has mispriced---and if so, does the signal carry economic
content?

\section{Residual formulation and the signal check}

We train a HistGradientBoostingClassifier on the \texttt{full+vegas}
feature set to predict \texttt{Win\_A}, isotonic on \texttt{val}, and
score each test fight by the residual
\[
r \;=\; p_{\text{model}}(\text{Win}_A \mid \text{features}, p_{\text{vegas},A})
       \;-\; p_{\text{vegas},A}.
\]
Positive $r$ means the model places more mass on corner A than the
market does.  The directional \emph{underdog-support} score flips
sign when B is the favourite: $s = r$ if $p_{\text{vegas},A} < 0.5$,
else $s = -r$.  A bet is placed on the Vegas underdog whenever
$s \ge \theta$.  On the full Vegas-aligned test slice, $s$ attains
ROC-AUC $0.588$ as an upset detector with a top-decile lift of
$1.56\times$ over the base rate.  The AUC is far from trading-grade
in isolation but is sharp enough to support the ROI simulation
below.  Two baseline residuals---Elo-minus-Vegas and
heatmap-minus-Vegas---both score AUC below $0.50$, i.e.\ they would
lose money as upset signals, so $s$ is not just a rediscovery of Elo
or of the $K{=}5$ heatmap.

Figure~\ref{fig:perm-importance} reports grouped permutation
importance \citep{breiman2001random} on the trained upset model.
Among non-market groups the \texttt{ae} group is the largest
contributor: shuffling the three AE columns drops validation AUC by
more than any other group.  The rolling-rate group is next, matching
the Chapter~\ref{ch:matchup} ablation.  The GMM soft posteriors are
near zero, indicating redundancy with the smoother autoencoder.
For upset detection specifically, the continuous embedding does
almost all of the style work; the discrete mixture does not.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{upset_perm_importance.png}
\caption{Grouped permutation importance on the residual upset
model: validation-AUC drop when a feature group is shuffled.
Among non-market groups the autoencoder embedding (\texttt{ae}) is
the single largest contributor; GMM soft posteriors are negligible.
Source: notebook~22.}
\label{fig:perm-importance}
\end{figure}

\section{ROI with bootstrap intervals}

The economic test is a flat-stake policy: for every test-slice
fight, bet one unit on the Vegas underdog whenever $s \ge \theta$,
receive the de-vigged decimal payout on a win, and lose the stake
otherwise.  Because the policy hinges on a threshold choice, we
report the ROI across a grid of thresholds with paired-bootstrap
95\% CIs from $2{,}000$ resamples of fights
(Table~\ref{tab:roi}).

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Simulated flat-stake ROI of the $s = r$ upset policy on
the Vegas-aligned test slice.  $P(\text{ROI}\le 0)$ is the empirical
probability of a non-positive resample ROI from $2{,}000$ paired
bootstrap draws.}
\label{tab:roi}
\begin{tabular}{cccccc}
\toprule
$\theta$ & $n_{\text{bets}}$ & Hit rate & Obs.\ ROI & 95\% CI & $P(\text{ROI}\le 0)$ \\
\midrule
$0.05$ & $674$ & $0.343$ & $+3.9\%$  & $[-7.8\%,\ +15.3\%]$ & $0.27$ \\
$0.10$ & $538$ & $0.349$ & $+9.4\%$  & $[-4.3\%,\ +23.1\%]$ & $0.10$ \\
$0.15$ & $411$ & $0.363$ & $+17.5\%$ & $[+1.5\%,\ +34.1\%]$ & $0.02$ \\
$0.20$ & $309$ & $0.369$ & $+23.4\%$ & $[+4.2\%,\ +43.8\%]$ & $0.01$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

At thin thresholds ($\theta \le 0.10$) the bootstrap CI spans zero;
at $\theta \ge 0.15$ the lower bound is positive, $P(\text{ROI}\le
0)$ drops below $2\%$, and the hit rate rises from $0.34$ to $0.37$
in the direction the residual's sharpness predicts.  Two caveats are
worth stating.  First, the simulation uses de-vigged rather than
tradable closing odds; real-money returns would be lower once the
book's vig is re-applied.  Second, $\theta = 0.15$ is reported as
the headline threshold because it is the smallest value at which the
CI excludes zero---not because it was pre-registered; a stronger
version of the experiment would lock $\theta$ on \texttt{val} before
touching \texttt{test}.  With those caveats, the thesis's defensible
claim is that on \emph{when} a favourite is vulnerable, style is the
dominant non-market contributor to the detection signal, the
residual produces a de-vigged ROI whose $95\%$ CI excludes zero at
$\theta \ge 0.15$, and market-free baselines are worse than random.
The style features are doing real work in the one place this
style-forward thesis most naturally expected them to: the tail of
matchups where the market's aggregate quality signal is dominated by
a stylistic asymmetry it does not price.

\chapter{Historical Upset Case Studies}\label{ch:cases}
% =====================================================================

A reader who has followed the empirical argument through
Chapter~\ref{ch:upset} is entitled to ask the concrete question: does
the framework, applied \emph{prospectively} to the largest moneyline
upsets in UFC history, produce residuals consistent with what
Chapter~\ref{ch:upset}'s threshold policy would have bet?
Notebook~24 applies the unified feature table to five canonical
upsets, fitting a fresh \texttt{HistGradientBoostingClassifier} on
every fight strictly preceding each event's date so that each
prospective probability is honestly leakage-free, even for upsets

Three readings of Table~\ref{tab:cases} are worth pulling out, and
each maps onto a specific claim made earlier in the thesis.

\paragraph{Cross-cluster style mismatches (Holm/Rousey,
Dillashaw/Barao).}  The two upsets whose canonical narrative is ``a
striker beat a grappler'' line up cleanly with the Chapter~\ref{ch:style}
representations.  Holm lands in Cluster~1 (active distance striker)
and faced Rousey, a pure judoka-grappler; Dillashaw in Cluster~3
(baseline generalist, with striking-heavy rolling rates) faced a
Cluster~1 striker in Barao.  These are the two cases where the
fighters' hard $K=5$ assignments differ and the thesis's style
vocabulary applies in its most familiar form.  The residuals on the
eventual winners---$+0.43$ and $+0.20$---are the two largest in the
table and exceed the Chapter~\ref{ch:upset} threshold of
$\theta = 0.15$ by wide margins.  These are the fights the framework
is built to catch, and it catches them.

\paragraph{Within-cluster hidden mismatches (Edwards/Usman,
Pena/Nunes).}  More striking for the thesis's central argument are the
two cases whose hard $K=5$ assignments \emph{agree}: Edwards and Usman
are both baseline generalists (Cluster~3), and Pena and Nunes are both
wrestlers (Cluster~4).  A framework that relied on discrete archetypes
alone would describe these as same-style matchups and see no stylistic
asymmetry.  The continuous autoencoder embedding disagrees.  Euclidean
divergences of $3.33$ and $3.49$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ sit in the top
quintile of the entire dataset, and the residuals on the eventual
winners---$+0.15$ and $+0.26$---both clear the Chapter~\ref{ch:upset}
threshold.  This is the cleanest concrete validation in the thesis of
the claim in Chapter~\ref{ch:style} that the continuous embedding
captures stylistic variation the hard mixture misses, and in
Chapter~\ref{ch:upset} that the \texttt{ae} feature group is the
single largest non-market driver of the upset residual.

\paragraph{Pre-odds historical (Serra/St-Pierre).}  UFC~69 predates
reliable Kaggle odds coverage, so no residual is computable.  Our
prospective model fit on all prior UFC fights assigns Serra only a
$5\%$ chance---the framework agrees with the contemporaneous
near-unanimous expectation of a St-Pierre win.  Serra's subsequent
first-round KO is a variance event the pre-fight features cannot have
predicted, and the framework correctly does not pretend otherwise.
This is, equally importantly, an honest \emph{null} for the framework:
our story is not that every famous upset was signalled; it is that
when the features do produce a residual in the bettable tail, the
signal is economically meaningful over the test window.

Taken together, the five case studies function as a concrete anchor
for the population-level ROI result of Chapter~\ref{ch:upset}.  The
four post-2010 cases all have residuals at or above $\theta = 0.15$;
the one historical case that the framework is silent on is also the
one in which the framework should be silent.  The case studies also
double as pointwise evidence for the continuous-over-discrete style
argument of Chapter~\ref{ch:style}: two of the four upsets the
framework flags (Edwards/Usman and Pena/Nunes) are invisible to the
hard $K=5$ clustering and visible only through the autoencoder
embedding that Chapter~\ref{ch:upset} identifies as the largest
non-market contributor.

\chapter{Discussion and Conclusion}\label{ch:conclusion}
% =====================================================================

The three empirical chapters answer the thesis's three questions
with answers of visibly different strength.  \emph{Who wins} is the
hardest question and the one where style helps least: the feature
stack lifts held-out AUC from $0.600$ to $0.670$ above a $Z$-score
baseline, and edges Vegas itself by $0.023$ AUC (95\% CI
$[+0.001,\ +0.045]$, $p = 0.04$) once $p_{\text{vegas},A}$ is added
as a feature, with a paired null result on hybrid-versus-specialist
win rates sharpening the picture.  \emph{How fights unfold} is where
style becomes a first-class signal: top-quintile autoencoder
divergence raises the finish rate from $\approx 0.47$ to
$\approx 0.56$ (the thesis's cleanest positive style effect), and a
hierarchical method-of-victory decomposition cuts six-way log loss
by $\sim 10\%$.  \emph{When a favourite is vulnerable} is where
style carries the most practical weight: the autoencoder embedding
is the largest non-market contributor to a residual upset detector
(ROC-AUC $0.59$, top-decile lift $1.56\times$), and a de-vigged
flat-stake policy returns $+17.5\%$ on $411$ bets at $\theta = 0.15$
with a bootstrap $95\%$ CI of $[+1.5\%,\ +34.1\%]$ and
$P(\text{ROI} \le 0) \approx 0.02$.  The single-sentence summary is
that \emph{style, learned directly from fight data, matters least
for who wins, more for how the fight ends, and most for when the
market is wrong}---a more specific and testable version of ``styles
make fights'' than the cliché usually supports.

Three limitations shape how far these results carry.  The UFCStats
scrape omits non-UFC fights, so extensive pre-UFC records---common
for championship entrants---do not enter our rolling aggregates;
Vegas coverage is partial and skewed to recent events, so every
Vegas-feature result is restricted to a subset.  The autoencoder is
trained on career means and therefore represents style-as-identity
rather than style-as-evolution: fighters whose styles change
mid-career are modelled as stationary and regress toward whichever
archetype dominates their aggregate record, which likely flattens
the hybrid-versus-specialist test of Chapter~\ref{ch:style}.  And
the test slice, though event-ordered, is a single $20\%$ window
rather than a multi-year out-of-time test; the ROI simulation uses
de-vigged odds and ignores line movement, stake sizing, and
bookmaker limits, and $\theta = 0.15$ was not pre-registered.

The most valuable single extension is therefore a pre-registered
out-of-time validation: refit the pipeline using only events
through 2022, lock $\theta$ on \texttt{val} in advance, and
evaluate on 2023 and 2024 separately.  If the upset ROI survives
that test, the framework transitions from defensible statistical
evidence into something approaching a deployable signal.
Complementary directions are quantile regression for the
heavy-tailed dynamics targets; sequence models of style
evolution---a first pass with a Siamese GRU
\citep{bromley1994signature,cho2014learning} over the last eight
pre-fight rounds did not beat the HGB baseline (test AUC $0.58$
versus $0.65$), likely a sample-size issue that a non-UFC-history
fix could address; and a causal pass at the style-pair heatmap of
Section~\ref{sec:heatmap} that disentangles style from correlated
attributes (reach, camp, age cohort) via matching or
instrumental-variable designs to sharpen claims about \emph{why}
certain styles beat certain others.  The contribution of this
thesis across all three is a compact, leakage-free style
representation that carries real non-market information about the
parts of MMA the cliché usually gets wrong.  All analysis code,
the unified feature table, and the paired-bootstrap scripts are
released alongside this manuscript; see \texttt{README.md} and
\texttt{run\_pipeline.py} in the project root for the full artefact
map.
